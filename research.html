Overview


My research activities address software engineering with issues related to distribution, composition, interaction, and a specific focus on behavioural descriptions. Behavioural descriptions are often found under different names such as behavioural interfaces, service conversations, or business processes.

I am also particularly interested in the use and the integration of formal methods  within the software development processes. This includes activities such as design, verification, software adaptation, automatic composition, and testing. The objective is twofold: first, to increase the confidence one may have in a software system and, second, to foster automation and tool-support of the development processes.

Applicative domains are important in my work, which I apply to component and services frameworks and to business models and processes.

Research Topics


  • Adaptation

  • Composition

  • Verification

In a context where systems are built (totally or partially) in a bottom-up way, one has to face sub-system mismatch. This issue typically arises when reusing components and services that have been developed by different third-parties, or by interconnecting enterprise systems. As a result of mismatch, system-level goals may fail to be reached, or deadlocks may occur during execution. Software adaptation addresses such issues by generating software pieces called adaptors that act in-between components, services, or enterprise systems. Adaptation is performed non-intrusively (externally) by adaptors, thus avoiding to change or to evolve the mismatching sub-systems. Going further than ensuring deadlock freeness, system-level properties can be enforced by the adaptors. In my work, I propose software adaptation techniques supporting different interface description levels (operations, behavioural, semantics).

The composition of reusable components or services, and the interconnection of enterprise systems enables, in theory, to gain time and money while developing added-value systems. In practice, if it is not automated, it is a complex and time-consuming activity: one must understand what reused entities do, how to reuse and combine them with others, and check that the resulting assemblies fulfill in practice composite system requirements.
Service composition tackles this issue with automatic (or at least partly automated) techniques for the discovery, selection, and assembly of reusable software entities into composite systems than can be deployed automatically. After being concerned about facilitating the enterprise software architects duties, automated service composition finds new applications with on-demand composition for the end-users and the software-as-a-service paradigm. Composition can be performed either in a centralized (orchestration) or decentralized (choreography) fashion. Composition also addresses repair in case composite systems get broken due to users' mobility, changing requirements, or component failure.
An important feature I support in my work are conversations in both available services and composition requirements. Further, I tackle both horizontal and vertical mismatch. The former comes from mismatch between inputs and outputs of chained services. The later comes from the different abstraction levels used in available services (described at the operation level) and in composition requirements (described using data-flows and capability level control-flows).

Verification and testing are complementary. The former is used to check if system specifications (e.g., business processes described in BPMN) correspond to what is required. The later is used to check if what is implemented corresponds to the specification. Testing is even more important in contexts where composite systems are built on top of black-box sub-systems such as service composition and enterprise systems interconnection. Active testing performs by interacting directly with the implementation. Passive testing may rely on system logs to be less intrusive and to better suit execution time constraints.
In my work I address both verification and testing, e.g., by verifying the realizability of BPMN choreography descriptions, and by testing (either in an active or passive way) the conformance of service choreographies and orchestrations.
